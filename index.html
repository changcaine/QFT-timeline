Here's a concise timeline of the history of Quantum Field Theory (QFT), highlighting key scientists and their accomplishments:

1920s–1930s: Foundations

  • 1927 – Paul Dirac:

    • Introduced the Dirac equation, a relativistic wave equation for the electron.

    • Predicted the existence of antimatter (positron).

  • 1928–1930 – Pascual Jordan, Eugene Wigner, Paul Dirac:

    • Developed second quantization, essential for field quantization.

  • 1934 – Enrico Fermi:

    • Developed the first quantum field theory of weak interactions (beta decay).

1940s: Renormalization and QED

  • 1947–1949 – Richard Feynman, Julian Schwinger, Sin-Itiro Tomonaga:

    • Independently developed Quantum Electrodynamics (QED), a relativistic quantum field theory of electromagnetism.

    • Solved infinities in calculations through renormalization.

  • 1949 – Freeman Dyson:

    • Unified the QED formulations of Feynman, Schwinger, and Tomonaga.

1950s–1960s: Expanding the Framework

  • 1954 – Chen-Ning Yang & Robert Mills:

    • Proposed non-Abelian gauge theories (Yang–Mills theory), foundational for modern particle physics.

  • 1961 – Sheldon Glashow:

    • Proposed a unification of weak and electromagnetic forces (precursor to the electroweak theory).

  • 1967–1968 – Steven Weinberg & Abdus Salam:

    • Developed the electroweak theory, unifying electromagnetism and weak interactions within a renormalizable gauge theory.

  • 1964 – Peter Higgs, François Englert, Robert Brout:

    • Proposed the Higgs mechanism to explain how particles acquire mass via spontaneous symmetry breaking.

1970s: The Standard Model

  • 1971 – Gerard 't Hooft & Martinus Veltman:

    • Proved the renormalizability of non-Abelian gauge theories, making the Standard Model viable.

  • 1973 – David Gross, Frank Wilczek, David Politzer:

    • Discovered asymptotic freedom in Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD), explaining the behavior of quarks at high energies.

  • Mid-1970s – Development of QCD:

    • Formulated as a non-Abelian gauge theory of the strong interaction (based on SU(3) symmetry group).

1980s–1990s: Advanced Concepts

  • 1980s – Kenneth Wilson:

    • Applied the renormalization group to QFT, revolutionizing the understanding of scaling and phase transitions.

  • 1983 – Discovery of W and Z bosons (CERN):

    • Confirmed electroweak theory predictions.

  • 1990s – Development of Effective Field Theories (EFT):

    • Used to handle physics at different energy scales (e.g., Chiral Perturbation Theory).

2000s–Present: Unification, Gravity, and Experiment

  • 2004 – David J. Gross, H. David Politzer, and Frank Wilczek win the Nobel Prize for QCD.

  • 2012 – ATLAS and CMS Collaborations at CERN:

    • Discovery of the Higgs boson, confirming the Higgs mechanism.

  • Ongoing – Quantum Gravity and Beyond Standard Model:

    • Efforts continue in string theory, loop quantum gravity, and conformal field theory.

    • The AdS/CFT correspondence (Juan Maldacena, 1997) offers insights into quantum gravity.
